Core NF Eff strategy

Before I start the game review, I want to first go over some core strategy and principles that I use when playing. This section turned out to be quite long, so feel free to skip ahead.

Most moves fall into two different categories -

  • Protected squares
  • Opening predictions

Protected squares

The foolproof way to tell if a square is protected is to look at the 8 surrounding squares and ask yourself - “could this be an opening”. If the answer is always no, then the square is protected. This works, however most of the time it is overkill. Instead, I look out for the below mine patterns (and rotations/reflections).

Here, dark grey indicates the area of the board that is already solved* and green represents the squares that are protected from being part of an opening.

*The dark grey squares don’t have to be exact, I’ve just included them to help show that these mine patterns only protect from openings occurring in a particular direction (in the case of this diagram they protect from openings coming in from the top right). We still have to be careful when looking for possible openings coming from the other direction (in this case, the bottom left).

Sometimes, a combination of the above patterns has to be used. For example, green here is protected, since both of the below combinations of mines are able to protect it.

It’s good to get into the habit of always clicking all of the known-to-be-safe protected squares before taking any risks.

Opening predictions

There are some general rules/principles that can be used when deciding where to go for an opening prediction. The choice depends a lot on what the board looks like, so these rules can be broken when it makes sense to do so.

  1. The fewer tiles required for a square to be an opening, the better. Green is better than cyan in the below screenshot as it requires 4 squares to be safe in order for it to be an opening whereas cyan requires 6 squares to be safe for this.

  2. It is preferable to go for openings where the square you are clicking on is known to be safe. Here, red is a known mine. Green is better than Cyan as it is known to be safe, so you avoid the [mine density]% risk of blasting. You may still lose a click if orange is a mine and cyan is an opening. However, this is an acceptable risk since the chance of this happening is less than [mine density]% since even if orange is a mine, you may still be saved if there are mines above it that prevent the opening.

  3. It is preferable to go for moves that can be “pulled” towards solved areas. Green is better than cyan in the below screenshot. They both have the same chance of giving an opening, but if instead they open a number, then green would enable you to uncover the yellow squares, and locate the mine(s) that are blocking the opening, whereas cyan would just let you uncover the brown square, which isn’t as useful. I will discuss “pulling” more later on in the document.

  4. Compare moves by thinking about where the mines and openings could be that would mess things up. It’s possible to go very far with this, as in theory, you could calculate all the different probabilities of situations that can arise, and use this to weigh up different opening predictions. Most of the time this isn’t necessary. In the below screenshot, I consider the green and the cyan opening predictions. For the green prediction, I note that there are 2 (orange) squares that could lose a click if they contain a mine and white is an opening. For the cyan prediction, there are 4 (orange) squares that could lose a click if they have a mine. So, I would judge green to be better than cyan as there are more ways that cyan can waste a click.

  5. Try to close down the board. This is quite a general principle, so I don’t have a screenshot to go along with it. It’s a good idea to try to approach regions of the board from all sides as this makes them easier to deal with. Openings have a slight tendency to travel along the edge of the board as they require 6 squares to be safe rather than the usual 9. You can use this to your advantage and try to enclose regions of the board. In practice, this can be quite hard to do as often you just have to go where the game takes you.
  6. Think about the bigger picture. I wasn’t sure exactly how to phrase this principle, but it is important. Cyan here seems like a very natural opening prediction to make. However, it provides no additional information about the board, so it could be better approached from a different direction. If, after solving the rest of the board, we discover that brown is a mine, then cyan can no longer be an opening and we would save ourselves from having to take an unnecessary [mine density]% risk with cyan. This example is quite specific, however the principle applies in lots of different situations. Whenever you make a guess, think about how it improves the state of the board - will it help solve an important area of the board? Is it likely to lead to lots of progress or is it in a closed off area that is better left for minecount?

  7. Don’t forget about the edge of the board. In particular, I mean moves like in the screenshot below, it’s easy to overlook them, but they can rescue you in difficult situations when nothing else looks good.

The mindset

The mindset of no flag efficiency depends on what your goal is. If you are dead set on achieving expert 100%, then this will naturally lead to a different strategy than if you are aiming for, say, above 95%. When aiming for 100%, you should treat losing a click to an opening and clicking on a mine as being equivalent. They both require a reset.

When aiming for 100%, you may find yourself going hours without having any close attempts, so I would encourage you to make the most of any attempts that look promising. This means slowing down to avoid misclicks/mistaken logic and carefully evaluating the order of which to play moves to minimise overall risk taken.

I would encourage you to minecount towards the end of games whenever there is a chance that knowledge of the minecount may lead to making a different decision. I use the paint bucket tool in jspaint for this and I change colour every 10 mines. Remember that expert has 99 mines instead of 100, so if you are counting in 10s the last batch will have 9 mines - I’ve made this mistake before.

If we assume that expert NF 100% happens once per 50 hours of playtime, then taking an unnecessary 80% safe guess right at the end of a winning game will on average set you back by 10 hours.

MSCoach has run some simulations with his solver on expert NF 100% Eff. He found that it won around 1 in 4,444 games using a 4-corner start (more info can be found on his profile ).

To give a comparison with intermediate - I can get 100% around 1 in every 7 games on intermediate when playing as carefully as possible. I once did this 6 times in a row !

Preparation guesses

Sometimes, in NF efficiency, there will be times where you may want to get more information about the board before going for an opening prediction. I want to cover a common example of this before going back to the review.

We consider the above example, which happens to be from our game, but we will treat this as if it could be from any game (with any density).

You may ask - is it better to go for cyan or green?

Cyan

Cyan is a guess with a [mine density]% chance of blasting (if you want to know why this is the case, you should watch Mine Buoy’s video on probability). The idea behind cyan is that it gives information on whether any of the grey squares contain a mine. If cyan reveals a 1, you can then click green (hoping for an opening), and if it reveals a 2 or 3 you will have to go for a backup guess somewhere else (such as the square immediately to the left of green).

Green

The idea with green is that whilst it is more immediately risky, it means that you only need to take one guess instead of two. Green has a [mine density]% chance of blasting just like cyan, but it has extra risk posed by the grey squares, as if these contain a mine, then it’s possible for green to be on the edge of an opening. Green also requires hoping for a less likely opening, this is because green being an opening requires 6 squares to be safe, whereas in the good case for cyan (that is where cyan reveals a 1) we only need 4 squares to be safe to give an opening.

I like to call guesses such as cyan “preparation guesses” because you are taking an additional risk in order to (hopefully) improve another guessing spot (in this case green).

So, which is the better guess? Cyan or green? The answer is that it primarily depends on the board density.

I’ve done some rough calculations on green and cyan, which inform my following commentary on them. There is a section in the appendix where I go through my calculations and reasoning in depth. As a reminder, intermediate has 15.6% density, and expert has 20.6% density.

For densities below 15%, you should choose cyan. This is because at lower densities, openings are more common, so the grey squares provide relatively more of a threat to the green guess. When the grey squares contain mines, it’s quite likely that there will also be an opening to the left of green causing it to lose a click. It is worth going for cyan to remove the threat posed by the grey squares.

For densities in the 15%-20%* range, things are less clear. Green wins out in short term survivability, but this is counteracted by the fact that cyan is more likely to lead to openings in the short term. I think both guesses are within a few percentage points of each other when combining these factors, so I can’t outright say which is the better option. For intermediate, I tend to choose cyan, but for anything denser I tend to choose green as my playstyle is quite heavy on opening predictions (openings tend to give a lot of progress, and if a board doesn’t have lots of openings, then it’s a bad board).

For densities in the 20%-25% range, you should click green because the high density means there isn’t as much of a threat of it losing a click to the edge of an opening.

For densities above 25%, you should start considering other options, such as playing safe in the hopes that there are no openings to bump into.

*I don’t have an exact switching point as this depends on the trade-off between likelihood of surviving a guess and likelihood of getting an opening, which is hard to put a number on.

I’d sum up my advice on preparation guesses as follows: look out for them, favour them on low densities or when the guess itself has low risk or gives a large amount of information, but be wary of the fact that you are taking an additional risk that may lead to no real progress.

Pulling and the rule of treating clicked squares as mines

Let’s consider the above situation more closely, assuming that we just revealed the pink 1.

There is a mental shortcut that can be used in this situation which only applies when playing for 100% efficiency. The mental shortcut is that you can pretend that the square you clicked on is a mine. Here a clicked square refers to a square that was clicked on individually rather than being part of an opening.

I’ll show this with diagrams first before explaining the logic behind this.

First consider the image on the left. It should be easy to see in this case that the mines protect all the yellow squares. These squares cannot be part of an opening.

Now consider the image on the right. Using the mental shortcut, my claim is that the exact same yellow squares are still protected. This turns out to be true, but only under the assumption that we are playing for 100%.

Why does this work? One way to understand this is to realise that when we are going for 100%, mines and clicked squares interact with openings in the same way. The “zero” tile of an opening can never be next to a mine (because then it wouldn’t be a “zero” tile). Similarly, the zero tile can never be next to a clicked square (purple in the diagram) as otherwise we would have already lost a click to an opening (which is equivalent to losing the game when going for 100%).

An alternate way to phrase this is - suppose we click on the yellow squares and they do happen to be on the edge of an opening losing us clicks, this would be acceptable as it means we have already lost a click with the clicked square (purple) so we were never going to get 100% on this board anyway.

It’s possible to estimate probabilities of certain risks to help judge them. The below diagram shows what would be required for green to lose a click to an opening.

The requirement for there to be a bad opening (white) is as follows:

The top mine should be in orange (½ chance)

The mines touching the 4 should be in orange (⅓ chance)

Four floating cells should be safe (1 - [mine density] chance per safe cell).

So the chance of this is ½ * ⅓ * (1 - [mine density])^4. We can either manually minecount to get the mine density, or assume it is somewhere around 1 in 5 (20.625% on expert to be precise). Long story short, it is unlikely for green to lose a click to an opening (around 7% chance assuming normal density).

So that should be the end of it, right? Green is equally as likely as the other options to be a mine but has good follow-up and very low risk of losing a click to an opening.

Some of you may have noticed that I haven’t mentioned green square’s twin.

The green square has a twin marked T above. The fact that green has a twin is another advantage of it that I didn’t draw much attention to before. T gives useful information that either improves the cyan guess or tells us to go somewhere else.

Strictly speaking, I should’ve clicked on T before I clicked on the left-green square. This is because T is part of the same guess as the left-green square, but is less exposed than it. Whenever left-green is safe, T will also be safe, but T doesn’t have the risk of losing a click to an opening.

High density strategy

As you approach the end of the board, it is often sensible to work out what the remaining mine density is. The best way to play a high density endgame is often quite different from how you would play a low density endgame. I find high density strats to be one of the harder parts of NF efficiency. There is no exact percentage where “high density” starts - I usually start to consider high density strats as the density gets close to 25% and above.

I’ve generated some typical 30x30 boards at different densities to give a rough idea of how these densities usually look. You should be able to see that openings start to make up much less of the board as the density increases. Openings are still reasonably common around the corners/edge of the board. I chose games close to the average 3bv for these boards, but the numbers given in the table will still differ from their true values.

I will give some general tips about high density before returning to the review.

  • You should lean towards going for squares that are known to be safe, even if they have some risk of being on the edge of an opening.
  • You should still be cautious around squares that are very likely to be openings (such as squares on the edge of the board).
  • You should try to pick squares that give good information. Be aware that playing safely often gives less information than predicting openings, and may need multiple risks to be taken for the same amount of progress.
  • Always think about how a move should be followed up. Before clicking any square, you should know where your next click will be for each possible number that could be revealed.
  • Think about how your guesses affect the remaining density. This matters more when there aren’t many squares/mines left. Guessing squares that touch many floating squares will give more knowledge about the remaining density. Also, playing for openings in one part of the board will make other parts of the board more dense on average. This is because if you get an opening in one part of the board, all the mines that could’ve been blocking that opening now must be elsewhere. The reverse is also true, that playing for there to not be openings will reduce the density elsewhere.
  • When playing safely, you may end up leaving some squares exposed to the risk of losing clicks. Keep track of these squares and avoid moves that increase the risk of these exposed squares losing clicks. Instead try to play moves that work with these at risk squares. I give a strategy for how to leverage at-risk squares in the “virtual mines” section of this guide.

The above example shows a common mistake when dealing with exposed squares. We can see that the “3” is not part of any already revealed openings and is also not protected by any mines, so it has potential to lose clicks to a future opening. It might be tempting to go for an opening prediction on purple, but this would be a mistake. If purple gives an opening, then it’s very likely that the opening will expand to also include the “3” which means that a click was wasted.

I’ll first compare cyan and green, and then I will come back to purple. Blue has been ruled out as it only makes sense on low density.

When playing by instinct, I would typically go for cyan, which is quite a natural move in lower density situations where green has the big risk of being ruined by the 50% chance of a mine being to its bottom-right. As this was a high-density and high-stakes situation, I examined the options more closely.

Cyan

  • The chance of being a mine is 0.3
  • The chance of being an opening (assuming it is safe) is 0.33

    This comes from the following expression where d is density: (1 - d)^3

  • The chance of losing a click to an opening (assuming cyan is safe) is 0.18

    This comes from the below calculation (colours based on diagram below)

    P(loses click given cyan is safe) = P(at least one mine in orange) * P(yellow safe) * P(red mine)

    In terms of density this is: (1 - (1 - d)^2) * (1 - d) * 1/2

Green

  • The chance of it being a mine is 0
  • The chance of it being an opening is 0.24

    This comes from (1 - d)^2 * ½.

  • The chance of it losing a click to an opening is 0.12

    This comes from the below (colours based on diagram below)

    P(green loses click) = P(yellow all safe) * P(red mine) = (1 - d)^4 * ½

So, seeing these numbers, it should be very clear that green is better on all measures except opening likelihood. In fact, contrary to what the numbers above might suggest, green is also better when it comes to opening likelihood.

I’ve been slightly deceptive in how I did these calculations. Whilst I stated that the chance of cyan being an opening is 0.33, this was conditional on cyan being safe. It would be better to look at the chance of cyan being an opening without necessarily assuming it is safe. When viewing it this way, the chance of cyan being an opening is P(cyan safe) * P(cyan is an opening given it is safe) = 0.23 . We had the equivalent figure of green being 0.24 , so green is very slightly more likely to produce an opening than cyan.

So green is better than cyan in all measures. I’ll now compare green to purple.

Purple

  • The chance of it being a mine is 0
  • The chance of it being an opening is 0
  • The chance of it losing a click to an opening is 0.11

    This comes from the diagram below

    P(purple loses click) = P (yellow all safe) = (1 - d)^6

So based on these numbers, I consider green to be better than purple. Purple is slightly less likely to lose a click than green, but green more than makes up for this as it has a 24% chance to give an opening (and even if it doesn’t give an opening, it will still give great progress).

A favourite pattern

Can you really call yourself a minesweeper player if you don’t have a favourite pattern?

Virtual mines

I will show a technique that can sometimes be used when you have squares that are at risk of losing a click to an opening. In the above screenshot, we are at risk of there being an opening on the white square that would cause us to lose clicks on the purple squares.

Where we left off was that we were in a precarious position. If the white square (or the square below it) is an opening, then we will lose clicks. The only way to prevent the white opening is to have a mine somewhere in orange (below diagram).

Provided that we are still on track for 100%, there will be a mine in the orange squares and so the green squares will be protected and safe to click on. This is the same concept as the virtual mines discussed in the previous section. In the game, I clicked one of the green squares and this led to a surprising amount of progress.

We clean up the protected squares and end up in the above situation. We have 4 floating mines in 15 floating squares (26.7% density).

An obvious question to ask is - which region should we start with. Usually, the answer is to start with the larger region, however these regions are quite similar sizes.

There are two more factors we can take into account when deciding which region to go for:

  1. How much benefit would the region get from having a more refined minecount. It can help to ask yourself questions such as: would I play the right region differently if I knew it had, say, 1 mine left vs 3 mines left. If a region would benefit significantly from knowing the minecount then it is better to leave it until last.
  2. How good are the guesses in this region. If a region has really good low risk guesses that are likely to solve the whole region, then it would suggest going for that region first.

I’ve labelled 4 candidate moves above. We have green and cyan in the left region. We have blue and dark green in the right region. Let’s consider these moves with the factors discussed above in mind.

As I will explain in more detail later on, the left region has a very strong guess with green. Minecount helps a little bit as if we were told that the number of mines in the left region was low (0 or 1), we would go for cyan instead. The left region does have a lot of information available though, so it is likely we can solve it without needing the extra help from minecount.

The right region is quite tricky. If we guess green or blue, we may end up getting stuck if they do not reveal a nice number. Minecount would be very helpful here. Low density would tell us to go for blue and high density would tell us to go for green. Having a precise minecount in the right region could also help us in situations where our guesses don’t yield direct logic.

With the above factors in mind, it makes sense to start in the left region taking the green guess, which is what I did in the game.

I’d like to go into a little bit more detail about why the green guess is so good. The idea behind green is that we are hoping that there is a mine preventing the opening in one of the 6 orange squares.

A quick sanity check confirms that green is a low risk option. The naive calculation would be as follows: green loses clicks if the 6 orange squares are safe, and the density is 26.7%, so the chance of it losing clicks is (1 - 0.267)^6 = 15%. This calculation is wrong. Each extra square required to be safe further increases the remaining density so the true chance of green losing clicks is only 9% (this can be worked out with combinatorics). With this calculation, we can see that green is much safer than taking a 26.7% chance on a floating square.

Usually most of the risks I take are from opening predictions as they tend to give better progress, however it is worth considering options like green as long as there is a clear idea behind them. The green squares provide a lot of information which should help us locate where the floating mines are in this region. It is a good idea to mentally play out the different situations of where the floating mines could be and whether we would be able to locate them.

For example, if we end up with something like the below image then we could click green and use the virtual mine method to locate which of the orange squares (hopefully) contains a mine.

With our green guess, we got very lucky. We were able to locate all 4 of the remaining mines without taking extra risk. I have marked one of the mines dark red as that would’ve been a very natural guess in this region (corresponding to cyan earlier), so it is fortunate that we left this region until the end and played it carefully.

As we have found all of the floating mines, we can then solve the right region with zero risk taken and complete the board.

Some miscellaneous tips

I have a few tips to mention here which I couldn’t fit in any other part of the guide.

One important skill is to be able to play the start of games fast, as that means that you can go through boards more quickly so that you come across more boards which have potential. One way to go faster is to know when to break away from the core strategy. For example, you can sometimes skip out on clearing protected squares if you know that those protected squares won’t give information that can change the best guess - you should just go for the best guess first. Also, when there are multiple similar guesses that are far away from each other, then unless the board looks very promising, it’s not always worth spending much time weighing up which is better as you’ll often end up having to do both guesses anyway, since information doesn’t usually travel very far in NF efficiency (at least for expert density).

An advanced skill to learn is to know when to minecount. Sometimes it is even possible to make a judgement by eye, for example, if you’ve revealed some very large openings and don’t see many high numbers on the boards then it is likely that the remaining density is on the high side, which can lead to playing in a different way.

It’s very important to mentally track which squares are at risk of being on the edge of an opening, as if these squares get hit by an opening then you’d know that you need to reset. There is a cheap trick for this, which is to take screenshots of at-risk squares, although I’d encourage practising remembering these squares instead.

How do you get a feel for how doable 100% NF expert is? I first got a feel for this by playing expert sized boards but with a lower number of mines, starting with 30x16/70 and then progressing on to 30x16/80, 30x16/90 and finally 30x16/99 (true expert), each size taking longer than the previous. I also recommend a similar strategy for other categories in minesweeper such as when trying to improve a time PB.

Appendix: Preparation guess analysis

Here I go a bit deeper into the theory behind preparation guesses at different densities. I’ve included this in the appendix as it’s not necessary for understanding the guide. It’s quite a maths heavy section, and there is a decent amount of uncertainty in my conclusion due to the difficulty of comparing different types of progress (e.g. surviving vs finding openings). There may still be some value in seeing how I approach this. And I would love to hear if anyone has new ideas on how to weigh up the various strategies.

As a reminder, a preparation guess is when you take an additional risk with protected squares in order to improve a potential opening prediction guess. We’ll revisit the example from earlier.

Here green is an opening prediction, and cyan is a preparation guess with a [mine density]% chance of blasting. We are considering whether it is better to go for the green guess or the cyan guess, and how this decision is affected by the mine density.

I’ll break down these guesses by considering the initial risks that apply to each, and also what the chance of getting an opening is from each. There is a third factor which I consider to be very important, which is that guesses give progress even if they do not immediately lead to openings. This “non-opening” progress may be realised in the short term, if perhaps some information gained leads to better opening predictions a few moves later. “Non-opening” progress may also be realised much later if information gained only becomes useful when a lot more of the board is revealed. Either way, this type of progress is very hard to quantify, so it’s not something that I will go into much with my analysis, though it is something that should be kept in mind when drawing conclusions.

Comparison of initial risk

When comparing risks, keep in mind that when going for 100%, losing a click and clicking a mine should be equally treated as losses. The below table shows the main risks that apply to green but not cyan and vice versa

Green Guess Cyan Guess
Green will lose a click if we have both of the below conditions
  1. There is at least 1 mine in orange
  2. All yellow squares are safe
 Cyan will blast if red is a mine
If cyan reveals a 2 (or 3) then a backup guess will be required such as purple, which has similar risks to the green guess (may lose a click if there is a mine in orange and all yellow are safe)

Take a moment to think about which of these initial risks is more likely to happen and how this might be influenced by board density.

Comparison of opening likelihood

Similarly, the table below shows some opening predictions that apply to green or cyan.

Green Guess Cyan Guess
White can be an opening if all 5 yellow squares are safe If cyan reveals a 1 then white can be an opening if all 3 yellow squares are safe
If cyan reveals a 2 (or 3) then a backup guess will be required which has a chance of producing an opening

Now that we have some idea of which factors affect each guess, it’s worth running some numbers to see how much these matter at different densities. I’ve included the image of where the guesses are again for ease of reference.

The formulas below are for the initial risk of each guess (either blasting or losing a click). I’m using the letter d to represent density.

Risk of green = chance green is a mine or it is safe but loses a click to an opening

Risk of green = P(green is mine) + P(green is safe) * P(green touches opening)

Risk of green = d + (1 - d) * (1 - (1 - d)^2) * (1 - d)^5

Risk of cyan = chance cyan is a mine or it is safe but the next move blasts/touches opening (the next move is either green or going for a backup guess)

Risk of cyan = P(cyan is mine) + P(cyan is safe) * [P(Grey is empty) * P(Green is mine) + P(Grey not empty) * P(square to left of green is mine or loses click to opening)]

Risk of cyan = d + (1 - d) * [ (1 - d)^2 * d + (1 - (1 - d)^2) * { d + (1 - d) * (1 - (1 - d)^2) * (1 - d)^5 } ]

I’ve plotted the initial risks below. The risk for the green guess is shown by the green line, and the risk of the cyan guess is shown by the blue line.

initial risk graph

We can see that the lines cross around 9% density, and after that the green guess has much less initial risk. We also note that around 20% density, the preparation guess (cyan) is almost 10% riskier than the border guess (green). So initial risk favours the preparation guess on very low density and the border guess on higher density.

Lets now do the same for the chance of each guess giving an opening

Chance of green opening = P(green is safe) * P(green is opening)

Chance of green opening = (1 - d) * (1 - d)^5

Chance of cyan opening = chance cyan is safe and we get an opening after (either from green or from a backup guess depending on what cyan reveals)

Chance of cyan opening = P(cyan is safe) * [P(Grey is empty) * P(Green is opening) + P(Grey not empty) * P(square to left of green is opening)]

Chance of cyan opening = (1 - d) * [(1 - d)^2 * (1 - d)^4 + (1 - (1 - d)^2) * (1 - d)^6]

I’ve plotted these below. The chance that green is an opening is given by the green line and the chance for cyan is shown by the blue line.

opening chance graph

We can see that the cyan guess tends to have around 2-3 percentage points higher chance of giving an opening than the green guess. This is what makes it so tricky to compare the two moves. For densities we are interested in (10% - 25%), the preparation guess has more initial risk, but a higher chance of producing an opening.

So a key question is how to weigh this up. How much is it worth taking extra risk (blasting/losing a click) for a higher chance of an opening. We do know that openings are incredibly valuable in NF Eff, as they are one of the best ways to make progress on a board.

One approach could be to look at the ratio between opening chance and risk taken. That is, for every 1% risk taken, how much chance of an opening do we get out of that. All we are doing here is dividing the chance of an opening by the initial risk of the guess. We should keep in mind that this approach isn’t taking into account other forms of progress, but we can consider that later. In the next graph, I look at this ratio, again using a green line for the green guess and a blue line for the cyan guess.

opening/risk ratio graph

With this new graph, we can see that the crossover point has shifted upwards to 11% (recall that when we were just looking at initial risk, the crossover was at 9%). So if we purely consider the opening/risk ratio it would suggest taking the border guess (green) for densities above 11% and going with the preparation guess (cyan) for densities below 11%.

In reality, the crossover point will be higher. This is because the cyan guess gives better progress than green outside of what we have already considered with trivial openings. As an example, we could end up with something like the below on the cyan guess.



Whilst cases like this aren’t accounted for in our formulas, it is clear that if we get something like this it would lead to further progress as we have some nice options for additional opening predictions. It’s very hard to take into account all of the different ways that the cyan guess can give extra “non-opening” progress.

In the end it comes down to a judgement call for how much to value the extra progress from cyan. I would encourage you to think about how the information from the cyan square could help out in possible games, I won’t go into this too much myself for the sake of brevity.

I’ll give my judgement on this, although it’s very subjective and unrigorous. I’ll assume the board density is 15% for reasons that will become clear. On 15% density, we survive the initial risk of the cyan guess around 70% of the time which is split between 40% for getting an opening and for 30% for getting no opening. This is admittedly very handwavy, but my judgement is that the extra unaccounted for information given by cyan is probably equivalent to 4% chance of getting an opening. If we were to edit our opening/risk ratio graph to boost cyan’s opening chance by 4% then we’d see the lines cross each other around 15%. This 4% figure is basically pulled from the air though, I have considered lots of different ways that the cyan guess can give progress to reach this figure, however I expect that everyone will judge this differently. Given that boosting cyan’s opening chance in this way changes the crossing point to 15%, then I would claim that you should go for cyan below 15% and green above 15%. However, the exact crossing point is very sensitive to how much cyan’s opening chance gets boosted by, for example if we instead boost cyan’s opening chance by 6% then the crossing point becomes 20% density.

There isn’t any strong takeaway from this section, and indeed there is a lot of uncertainty in my conclusions. I’ve mainly provided this to give some insight into how I view preparation guesses. It’s worth keeping in mind that preparation guesses should always be treated with caution, there are no hard rules, and every situation is different.
